We have found that a toxic substanceexisted inthe stomach in the case under consideration, which when injected into an animal produced a marked fall of blood pressure and slowing of the heart beat. This substance was not present in the normal gastric contents of health; and, moreover, after neutralisation of the tetany fluid, practically no fall of pressure was obtained. We do not know what this toxic substance is; it has a marked acid reaction. Whether the brown colour and chloroform-like smell of the alcoholic solution are due to this material we cannot say. It is soluble in alcohol and in normal saline solution. By its injection into animals itcauses either direct or reflex excitation of the cardio-inhibitory centre. It is, therefore, by no means improbable that other centres in the brain or cord are similarly excited.
